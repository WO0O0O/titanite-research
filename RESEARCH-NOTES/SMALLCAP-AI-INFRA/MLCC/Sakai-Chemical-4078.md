## SECTION 0 — THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ TEST

### Supply Chain Map

| Layer | Description |
|---|---|
| **Raw Materials** | Barium chloride (imported, primarily from China/global) + strontium compounds + process chemicals |
| **Sakai Chemical's Input** | Barium chloride + proprietary reagents |
| **Sakai Chemical's Output** | Ultra-high-purity barium titanate (BaTiO₃) dielectric powder via proprietary hydrothermal synthesis |
| **Downstream: Direct** | Tier 1 MLCC manufacturers: Murata Manufacturing, TDK, Taiyo Yuden, Samsung Electro-Mechanics, Yageo |
| **Downstream: Next layer** | PCB/module assemblers: Foxconn, Quanta, Wistron, Samsung |
| **End-use** | AI servers (Nvidia H100/GB200 platforms), 5G base stations, EV power electronics |

### If Sakai Disappeared Tomorrow

Murata, TDK, and Taiyo Yuden — who collectively supply ~65–70% of global MLCC output — would face an immediate shortfall in MLCC-grade dielectric powder. Murata alone consumes an estimated 25–35% of global high-purity BaTiO₃. The only credible replacement at scale within 12 months would be:
- **Nippon Chemical Industrial** (NCI, TSE: 4092) — covered separately in this research series
- **Fuji Titanium Industry** — smaller, partially captive to Murata JV
- **Shandong Sinocera** (China) — capable in standard grade, but lacks trust from Japanese OEMs for highest-purity grade; qualification required

**Time to qualify alternative supplier:** 18–36 months minimum for automotive/AI-grade applications.

### Competitor / Substitute Map

| Competitor | Estimated Global Share | Threat Level |
|---|---|---|
| Sakai Chemical | ~28% (MLCC-grade powder, world's largest) | — |
| Nippon Chemical Industrial | ~15–18% | Moderate (see NCI report) |
| Fuji Titanium Industry | ~10–12% | Low-moderate (partially captive to Murata) |
| Shandong Sinocera (China) | ~15–20% | Growing, but quality/geopolitical constraints |
| KCM / Vibrantz (Ferro) | ~5–8% | Low |

**Top 5 players represent ~48–52% of global barium titanate revenue.** The high-purity MLCC-grade segment is effectively a **duopoly/tight oligopoly** between Sakai and NCI at the premium quality tier.

### Strait of Hormuz Number

Approximately **25–30% of global MLCC-grade barium titanate powder supply passes through Sakai Chemical.** Including premium hydrothermal synthesis specifically (the process required for advanced, sub-100nm particle MLCCs), Sakai's share is likely higher — potentially **35–40% of the hydrothermal premium tier.**

### Switching Costs

Minimum **18–24 months** to qualify an alternative dielectric powder supplier in consumer-grade MLCCs. For automotive and high-reliability applications: **24–36 months**, requiring AEC-Q200 re-qualification and full design revalidation. The switching cost is effectively prohibitive in the near term.

**Verdict: ✅ PARTIAL CHOKEPOINT**

Rationale: Sakai is the world's largest supplier of MLCC-grade dielectric powder by volume (~28% global share). However, it is not a monopoly — NCI and Fuji Titanium exist as alternatives, and Chinese producers are credible at the standard tier. The "Hormuz" status applies specifically to the *ultra-high-purity hydrothermal synthesis tier* used in advanced MLCCs for AI and automotive. This is a significant but qualified chokepoint.

## SECTION 1 — WHICH AI INFRA BOTTLENECK DOES IT SOLVE?

**Bottleneck: Substrates and Materials — Specifically MLCC Dielectric Powder**

The physical constraint addressed by Sakai Chemical is the **materials bottleneck in MLCC production for AI server power delivery and decoupling**.

**The mechanics:**
- A single Nvidia H100 or GB200 AI accelerator contains approximately **3,000–10,000 MLCCs** for power filtering, decoupling, and voltage regulation
- An AI server rack (8 GPUs + NVLink fabric) requires an estimated **500,000–1,000,000 MLCCs**
- Each MLCC's dielectric layer is made of sintered barium titanate — no viable substitute at scale
- As MLCC miniaturization advances (smaller case sizes, higher capacitance), particle purity requirements increase, concentrating demand toward Sakai-grade powder
- Global MLCC demand exceeded **1 trillion units annually** in 2025, with AI server MLCCs growing >30% YoY

**Hyperscalers affected by MLCC shortage:** AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Meta AI — all dependent on Nvidia/AMD GPU supply, which is downstream of MLCC supply from Murata/TDK, which is downstream of BaTiO₃ powder from Sakai.

**Quantified gap:** MLCC-grade barium titanate capacity expansion requires 2–3 years (new hydrothermal plant construction). Demand growth from AI servers is outpacing powder supply in the premium segment.

**Is Sakai a primary solver or peripheral beneficiary?** Primary solver — it sits at the atomic input layer of the MLCC supply chain. No premium MLCC can be made without dielectric powder of this grade. However, Sakai is 2 supply chain layers removed from the hyperscaler, weakening the direct linkage evidence.

**Score: 1/1** — The bottleneck is specific (MLCC dielectric materials), quantifiable (1T+ units/year, AI server MLCC demand +30% YoY), and Sakai is the world's largest primary solver at the materials layer.

## SECTION 10 — SECULAR AND CYCLICAL TAILWINDS

### Secular (10-year structural)

**Underlying structural driver:** MLCC densification in AI servers, 5G/6G infrastructure, electric vehicles, and autonomous systems. Each successive wave of electronics sophistication requires more MLCCs per device and higher performance per MLCC.

**Key data points:**
- Global MLCC shipments: >1 trillion units/year (2025), growing at ~8–12% CAGR

- Each EV requires 5,000–20,000 MLCCs vs. 1,000–3,000 in ICE vehicles

**Still valid in 2030+?** Yes. There is no substitute for BaTiO₃ as an MLCC dielectric at the scale and cost required by the electronics industry. Barium titanate has been the incumbent material for 70+ years and remains the only commercially viable option.

**Does demand survive a 30% AI capex cut?** Partially. EV and 5G demand would sustain MLCC growth even if AI capex declined 30% in a single year. The structural driver is diversified across multiple growth end-markets.

### Cyclical (1–3 year near-term)

**Industry upcycle:** MLCC inventory destocking cycle bottomed in 2023. FY2024 marked the beginning of recovery. AI server build-out (2024–2027) is creating demand levels not previously seen for high-end MLCCs.

**Trough:** MLCC market trough was 2022–2023 (global inventory correction post-COVID build-up). Recovery began clearly in H2 2023/FY2024.

**Duration:** The AI data centre build-out cycle is expected to run 2024–2028. EV adoption provides a second overlapping demand wave.

**Score: 1/1** — Both a clearly articulated secular trend (MLCC growth, AI/EV/5G structural driver) and a near-term cyclical recovery (post-2022 trough upcycle) are present simultaneously, with quantitative support from market data.

### Friend-Shoring / Domestic Content Benefits

Japan's government has been increasingly focused on semiconductor and electronic component supply chain security. Sakai, as the world's largest domestic BaTiO₃ producer, is strategically aligned with Japan's industrial policy. No CHIPS Act equivalent direct subsidy identified, but the geopolitical preference for domestic Japanese suppliers (vs. Chinese alternatives) among Japanese OEMs is a structural competitive advantage.

### Export Control Risk

Sakai's products (dielectric powders) are not subject to current US export controls. The products are exported to all major MLCC OEM regions. If US-China trade tensions intensified to the point of restricting Chinese access to Japanese electronic materials, Sakai would benefit from increased demand from non-Chinese OEMs while Chinese producers are cut off.

The combination of (a) diversifying away from Chinese raw material dependence, (b) being a Japanese domestic champion in a critical material, and (c) benefiting from friend-shoring preferences of Japanese OEMs makes the geopolitical environment net positive for Sakai's competitive position.
