## SECTION 0 — THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ TEST

**Supply chain map for LPKF's primary product: NEXAR LIDE 5000 (Through-Glass Via formation equipment)**

1. **Upstream:** Specialty glass manufacturers — Corning, SCHOTT, AGC, NEG, HOYA — supply ultra-thin, ultra-flat glass panels (typically 50–400µm). These are commodity inputs for LPKF's process; LPKF does not own this layer.

2. **LPKF's exact position:** LPKF takes bare glass panels and uses its patented two-step LIDE process — ultra-short pulsed laser modification followed by wet chemical etching — to create Through-Glass Vias (TGVs) with micron-level precision, no micro-cracking, no debris, and no thermal damage. Output: processed glass panels ready for copper metallisation and redistribution layer deposition. LPKF also provides Ablate 5000 (RDL ablation) and Bond 5000 (glass-to-glass bonding) — a complete production line.

3. **Directly downstream:** Metallisation and packaging houses — Absolics (SKC subsidiary, Georgia, USA), Samsung Electro-Mechanics, Intel's advanced packaging facilities. These organisations cannot produce glass core substrates without TGV formation. If LPKF equipment is absent, the substrate is a plain glass sheet.

4. **Hyperscaler end-use:** Glass core substrates replace organic substrates in AI accelerator packages. They enable larger package footprints, finer interconnects, superior thermal stability, and lower dielectric loss — requirements that are physical, not optional, for next-generation AI chips (NVIDIA Blackwell successors, AMD MI400 series, Intel's Gaudi roadmap). The end buyers are Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, AWS, and Meta, procuring through Tier 1 ODMs and directly from chip designers.

5. **If LPKF disappeared tomorrow:** No currently qualified alternative exists at production scale for the LIDE process specifically. Customers using LPKF equipment would need to re-qualify a substitute process from scratch — industry estimates put qualification cycles at 12–18 months minimum. Intel's glass substrate programme, Absolics's Georgia line, and Samsung's dual-source efforts would all face immediate hold-ups. The 2027 mass production timeline would slide to 2028–2029.

6. **Competitors and substitutes:**
   - **Philoptics (South Korea):** Direct-laser-drill TGV process, without the LIDE chemical etch step. LPKF publicly disputed Philoptics' claimed flexibility in September 2024. LPKF's LIDE patent was upheld by the European Patent Office in March 2025; Korean patents secured in September 2025. Philoptics has not publicly announced any volume production orders from a tier-1 customer.
   - **Canon, Yield Engineering Systems:** Peripheral roles in adjacent process steps; not direct TGV competitors at this stage.
   - **Plan Optik, Tecnisco:** Smaller niche players without the scale, IP portfolio, or customer base to displace LPKF.
   - **In-house development:** Theoretically possible, but the capital and IP barrier is substantial. No hyperscaler has announced an in-house TGV laser process.
   - **Market structure:** Near-duopoly trending toward monopoly, with LPKF holding the dominant position. Philoptics is the only credible challenger, and their process has not cleared the same IP validation milestones.

7. **Strait of Hormuz test:** LPKF has confirmed that over 80% of major global semiconductor players engaged in glass substrate development are using its equipment. Given the nascent state of the market — there are no other volume-production-qualified laser TGV systems — LPKF effectively controls the formation step for 80%+ of global glass substrate R&D and qualification activity, trending toward a similar share of production capacity as the market scales. No competing system has qualified at a tier-1 customer to the same degree.

8. **Switching costs:** Qualifying an alternative TGV process at a tier-1 semiconductor customer takes 12–18 months minimum, requires re-running yield and reliability studies across hundreds of process steps, and involves the customer's own downstream metallisation and assembly qualification — all of which is integrated to the LIDE output parameters. Switching costs are extremely high.

**Verdict: CHOKEPOINT.** LPKF is the single most qualified provider of the laser formation step that makes glass core substrates manufacturable. The constraint is not LPKF's readiness — the constraint is the downstream process readiness of its customers. That is the purest possible qualification-cycle chokepoint signature.

## SECTION 1 — WHICH AI INFRA BOTTLENECK DOES IT SOLVE?

**Bottleneck addressed: Advanced packaging — specifically substrate-level physical limitations blocking next-generation AI chip integration.**

Organic substrates, which underpin virtually all current AI accelerator packages, face hard physical limits as package sizes and via densities required for AI chips increase. At chiplet pitches below 10µm and package sizes exceeding 100mm×100mm, organic substrates exhibit warpage, CTE mismatch with silicon, and signal loss that cannot be engineered away. Glass core substrates offer near-zero CTE, dimensional stability at large formats, and low dielectric loss — properties that are physically necessary, not merely preferable, for the next generation of AI chips.

The constraint is quantifiable: the AI chip industry is moving to package sizes and interconnect densities that will require glass substrates, not organics, to achieve target yields. TSMC's CoWoS-G, Intel's glass substrate programme, and Samsung's advanced packaging roadmap all confirm this direction. LPKF's LIDE process is the only production-qualified method for creating the TGVs that make glass substrates electrically functional.

The hyperscalers affected: Google (TPU v6+), Microsoft (Azure Maia successors), AWS (Trainium/Inferentia roadmap), Meta (MTIA successors). All are dependent on advanced semiconductor packaging — and all are on a trajectory toward glass substrates in the 2027–2029 window.

**Score: 1/1.** The bottleneck is specific, physically constrained, and quantifiable. LPKF is a primary solver — without TGV formation capability, glass substrate manufacturing does not begin.

## SECTION 10 — SECULAR AND CYCLICAL TAILWINDS

**Secular (10-year structural):**

The shift from organic substrates to glass core substrates in advanced semiconductor packaging is a consequence of physics, not preference. Organic substrates cannot achieve the via density, dimensional stability, thermal performance, or signal integrity required for next-generation AI chiplets at the package sizes and interconnect pitches now being designed. This constraint does not disappear under any scenario short of a fundamental change in chip architecture.

The SEMI/GNC May 2026 report projects a 67.2% CAGR for the glass core substrate market between 2028 and 2040. This is a headline figure from an early-stage market and should be treated with appropriate scepticism — but even at one-third of that CAGR (approximately 22%), the market grows from near-zero to several billion dollars by 2035. LPKF, as the equipment provider for the formation step, captures a share of every new production line established.

This demand is irreversible in the sense that it is driven by the laws of physics applied to chip design. It survives a 30% AI capex cut for one year because glass substrate qualification programmes operate on 24–36 month cycles — a single year's capex reduction would delay, not cancel, the production ramp.

**Cyclical (1–3 year near-term):**

The cyclical driver is the advanced semiconductor packaging investment upcycle beginning in 2026–2027, following the qualification phase of 2023–2025. The trough in LPKF's advanced packaging order intake corresponds to the period during which customers were qualifying the process rather than ordering production systems. Q1 2026's first capacity expansion order marks the transition.

The upcycle is driven specifically by AI infrastructure capex, which continues to grow despite macro uncertainty — Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon have collectively committed hundreds of billions in data centre capex through 2027. Advanced packaging is a bottleneck in that supply chain.

**Score: 1/1.** Both secular (physics-driven substrate transition, 67.2% CAGR projection from SEMI) and cyclical (AI capex upcycle, glass substrate production investment entering ramp phase in 2027) are clearly present and compounding simultaneously.

_Evidence quality: Strong._

## SECTION 13 — ADVERSARIAL TESTING: STEEL-MAN THE BEAR CASE

**1. Thesis killer — single most credible scenario for failure within 24 months:**

A major tier-1 customer — most likely Intel or Samsung — publicly abandons its glass substrate programme or announces a multi-year delay due to yield problems in the downstream metallisation step. This would signal that the 2027 mass production timeline is unrealistic and would cause the market to re-price LPKF's advanced packaging option back toward zero. The glass substrate metallisation and assembly process steps are outside LPKF's control, and they are the current bottleneck, as management has explicitly acknowledged. A public failure at Absolics or Samsung Electro-Mechanics would be the specific trigger.

**2. Short report reconciliation:** No active short report. Not applicable.

**3. Substitute threat:**

Philoptics is the only credible TGV process competitor. Their direct-laser approach could — if they secure a major tier-1 qualification — erode LPKF's 80%+ market share in the glass substrate equipment space. The timeline for Philoptics to achieve tier-1 qualification at scale is estimated at 18–24 months from today, given that LPKF has been actively asserting IP and Philoptics has not publicly announced equivalent customer penetration. This is a real risk but not an imminent one.

**4. Concentration stress test:**

LPKF's current revenue is not concentrated in the advanced packaging segment — it is concentrated in legacy Electronics, Welding, and the collapsing Solar segment. Loss of the Solar segment entirely (which appears to be occurring) reduces FY2025 revenue from ~EUR 115 million to an estimated EUR 90–95 million for the remaining segments. This is already partially priced in. Loss of any single advanced packaging customer at this stage would have minimal impact on reported revenue (because advanced packaging revenue is pre-material). The stress scenario is not customer loss — it is thesis delay.

**5. Technology skip risk:**

The primary skip risk is if a next-generation AI chip architecture emerges that uses chiplets packaged in a fundamentally different format — e.g., silicon photonics at wafer scale, or monolithic 3D integration that bypasses substrate-based packaging entirely. This is a genuine long-term risk but is at least a decade away from threatening the 2027–2030 thesis window. In the near term, every major AI chip roadmap published by Intel, NVIDIA, AMD, and TSMC confirms the substrate-packaging approach.

**6. Balance sheet risk:**

- Equity ratio: 69.4% as of March 2026
- No equity raise in 24 months; ~24.5 million shares outstanding
- Syndicated loan extended through 2028
- Q1 2026 adjusted EBIT: EUR –5.7 million — cash burn is real but manageable
- North Star restructuring costs estimated at 3–4% of revenue in 2026 (approximately EUR 3–5 million)
- At EUR 17.1 million quarterly revenue and EUR –5.7 million EBIT, the company is burning cash. The question is whether the syndicated loan facility is sufficient to bridge to 2027 mass production orders.
- Runway: With a 69.4% equity ratio and extended credit through 2028, there is no imminent liquidity crisis. However, if production orders do not arrive in 2027, the 2028 target becomes strained.

**7. Structural vs. temporary:**

The LIDE chokepoint is structural — it is protected by patents, manufacturing know-how, qualification inertia, and the physics of the substrate transition. The 18-month window closing scenario applies if Philoptics reaches equivalent qualification before LPKF converts its existing customers to production orders. This is possible but currently unlikely given the IP enforcement posture and the 12–18 month qualification cycle for any alternative process.

**8. Capex cut scenario:**

If hyperscaler AI capex is cut 40%, advanced semiconductor packaging investment would likely decline 20–30% in the same period — but glass substrate production investment is a multi-year infrastructure decision, not a quarterly discretionary spend. Programmes already in qualification (Intel, Samsung, Absolics) are unlikely to be cancelled by a one-year capex cut. Production system orders might be deferred by two to four quarters. Revenue impact on LPKF's advanced packaging segment: delayed by one to two quarters; thesis intact.

**Overall bear case rating: MODERATE.**

The bull case survives because the substrate physics are real, the patent position is confirmed, the customer penetration is 80%+ and documented, and the balance sheet has runway through 2028. The risk is timing, not thesis validity. A 12-month delay in mass production translates to a 12-month delay in the re-rate, not a thesis failure.

## SECTION 14 — GEOPOLITICAL DIMENSION

1. **China supply chain exposure:** LPKF's glass substrate business is oriented toward non-Chinese customers — Intel (US), Samsung (Korea), Absolics (US). The existing legacy Electronics segment may have some China revenue, but advanced packaging customers are in the US and Korea. LPKF does not manufacture in China. Upstream glass suppliers (Corning US, SCHOTT Germany, AGC Japan, NEG Japan) are non-Chinese.

2. **Chinese export restriction exposure:** LPKF's LIDE equipment uses components that are not specifically subject to Chinese export restrictions. The technology itself is a laser process system — not a semiconductor lithography tool in the category covered by Dutch/US/Japanese export control regimes. No specific BIS Entity List implications identified.

3. **Friend-shoring / domestic content incentives:** Absolics is manufacturing in Georgia, USA, and benefits from CHIPS Act incentives. Intel's US-based advanced packaging investments are CHIPS Act-eligible. LPKF as a German company does not directly receive CHIPS Act funding, but its equipment is the enabler for CHIPS Act-funded substrate production — creating indirect demand pull from US industrial policy. The EU Chips Act separately supports European semiconductor manufacturing, which could benefit LPKF's home market.

4. **Export control risk to China:** LPKF's equipment is advanced laser processing systems for glass. It is not on the current CCL/EAR control list for semiconductor equipment in the same category as ASML or Applied Materials tools. No China export restriction exposure identified at current technology levels.

5. **National security / defence argument:** Glass substrates are being evaluated for defence electronics (high-reliability, miniaturised packaging). The US DoD interest in domestic advanced packaging creates a soft national security argument for LPKF's customer base, even if not a direct government contract relationship.

**Verdict: GEOPOLITICAL TAILWIND.** The glass substrate supply chain is being actively incentivised in the US and EU via CHIPS Act and EU Chips Act. LPKF's customers are non-Chinese. Its technology is not subject to Chinese export restrictions. The friend-shoring dynamic actively pulls demand toward LPKF-equipped fabs.
