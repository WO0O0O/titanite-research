## SECTION 0 — THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ TEST

**1. Layer directly upstream:** Raw process chemicals (etchants, plating baths, photoresists), mechanical and electrical components for production systems, vacuum and laser subsystems (TRUMPF collaboration for TGV glass processing).

**2. SCHMID's exact position:** Takes in raw inputs and manufactures integrated wet-process production lines (InfinityLine family), CMP systems (L+), panel-level plating systems (P+), and vacuum metallisation equipment. Delivers turnkey line solutions — not discrete components — to PCB fabricators, IC substrate manufacturers, and advanced packaging facilities. Also provides the "Any Layer ET" embedded trace process as a licensed/equipment-plus-know-how offering.

**3. Layer directly downstream:** High-end PCB manufacturers (primarily Asian), IC substrate producers (ABF substrate makers, glass-core substrate developers), and OSATs. These customers cannot run AI server board production lines or advanced packaging lines without the wet-process and plating equipment SCHMID provides.

**4. Trace to hyperscaler end-use:** PCB fabricators → AI server motherboards and HPC boards → data centre OEMs (Dell, Super Micro, HPE) → hyperscaler rack deployments (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, Meta). For the glass-core and advanced packaging line: glass substrate producers → IC packages for AMD, NVIDIA, Intel → GPU and CPU chips → AI training and inference infrastructure.

**5. If SCHMID disappeared tomorrow:** Customers with in-production SCHMID lines would face immediate production disruptions. Lead times on specialised wet-process equipment from alternative suppliers are 6–18 months. For Asian PCB manufacturers in the middle of capacity expansion for AI boards, loss of SCHMID would delay ramp timelines measurably. However — and this is the critical constraint on the chokepoint thesis — SCHMID does not hold 50%+ global market share in wet-process PCB equipment. Alternatives exist.

**6. Competitors and substitutes:**
- **MKS Instruments (Atotech):** The primary global competitor. Full-stack chemistry + equipment model, deeply embedded in advanced substrate roadmaps. Probably the strongest direct competitor in HDI and IC substrate wet processing.
- **Lam Research:** Extending semiconductor-grade panel processing into advanced packaging. Heavy capex, credible roadmap.
- **RENA Technologies:** German competitor in wet chemical processing, active in semiconductor and glass processing.
- **KLA (Orbotech division):** Inspection and imaging, complementary rather than directly substitutive in wet process.
- **Chemcut:** PCB-focused, lower-end of the technology range vs. SCHMID's AI-tier systems.

Market structure: **oligopoly**, not monopoly. SCHMID is a leading specialist, but Atotech/MKS is arguably the peer with comparable or stronger global positioning in the most critical substrate layers.

**7. Strait of Hormuz test:** No public figure for SCHMID's exact global market share in high-end HDI wet-process equipment exists. Industry analysts (Yole Group) indicate the advanced packaging equipment market has a handful of meaningful players. A reasonable estimate is that SCHMID commands 10–20% of global high-end HDI wet-process equipment shipments, with Atotech/MKS holding a comparable or larger share. For the panel-level packaging segment specifically (the highest-growth area), SCHMID may hold 20–30% given its longer specialisation in that format. This is a meaningful position, but not a Strait of Hormuz.

**8. Switching costs:** Customers integrating a SCHMID production line face 6–18 months of re-qualification with an alternative supplier, plus potential downtime and yield risk during transition. Switching costs are material but not prohibitive — particularly for customers who are still in design/planning phases rather than volume production.

**Required verdict: PARTIAL CHOKEPOINT**

SCHMID is a critical equipment supplier to the AI-server PCB and advanced packaging supply chain with meaningful switching costs. It is not a monopolist and does not control a chokepoint in the strict sense. The thesis rests on specialisation, deepening AI order momentum, and first-mover positioning in panel-level packaging and glass-core processing — not on irreplaceable control of global flow.

## SECTION 1 — WHICH AI INFRA BOTTLENECK DOES IT SOLVE?

**Bottleneck addressed:** Advanced packaging and AI server board PCB manufacturing capacity.

The AI infrastructure build-out requires AI server motherboards with high layer-count HDI structures — the boards inside every GPU rack and HPC server. Demand for these boards is growing in line with hyperscaler AI capex, which grew at 40%+ annually in 2023–2025 and remains above 20% in forward guidance from AWS, Azure, Google, and Meta. The key bottleneck is manufacturing capacity for these boards: the equipment to plate, etch, and process the increasingly fine-pitch, high-density interconnects required for next-generation AI chips is in limited supply and has lead times of 6–18 months.

SCHMID's InfinityLine H+ and V+ systems are the production tools enabling this manufacturing capacity expansion. Its L+ CMP system and the emerging glass-core substrate process (QuantumLine, AVACO plasma, TGV capabilities) address the next layer of the bottleneck: the transition from organic ABF substrates to glass-core packages, which is a 2027–2029 mass-production horizon.

SCHMID is a primary solver for the PCB/HDI equipment capacity bottleneck. For the glass-core substrate equipment bottleneck it is an early-stage player with credible technology but not yet at production scale.

**Quantification:** The global AI server PCB market is estimated to grow from approximately $8 billion in 2024 to $25 billion by 2028 (Yole Group estimates). Equipment spending for new capacity typically represents 15–25% of new production facility investment. At 15% equipment intensity and $25 billion in end-market revenue, the AI-server PCB equipment market is approximately $3–4 billion by 2028. SCHMID, at 10–20% share of relevant equipment, is addressing a $300–800 million opportunity within that market.

**Score: 1/1** — The bottleneck is specific, quantifiable, and SCHMID is a primary solver for the HDI/AI-server-board equipment segment.

## SECTION 8 — TECHNOLOGY LEADERSHIP / FIRST-MOVER ADVANTAGE

**Core InfinityLine wet-process systems:** SCHMID has a 160-year manufacturing heritage and decades in PCB wet-process equipment. The InfinityLine platform is established. However, Atotech/MKS has comparable depth. This is a duopoly-to-oligopoly market, not a first-mover monopoly.

**Panel-level packaging (PLP):** SCHMID is among the first to offer an industrialised PLP-capable horizontal processing system at formats up to 700×700mm. Competitors (Lam Research, RENA) are developing PLP-compatible tools but SCHMID has delivered production-ready systems first, at least at the large-format end. 12–18 month technology lead estimate in PLP horizontal processing, but this will compress as competitors scale their offerings.

**Glass-core substrate processing:** SCHMID offers TGV-capable QuantumLine and AVACO plasma systems. It partners with TRUMPF (a leading laser company) for TGV laser processing. This is an emerging field with multiple competitors: TRUMPF itself, LPKF (a direct competitor in laser glass processing, covered separately in this framework), and potentially Corning's internal process development. SCHMID is well-positioned but not uniquely positioned.

**"Any Layer ET" embedded trace:** A proprietary process that SCHMID claims enables full panel-level advanced packaging with ultra-fine RDL. Launched April 2026 — too early to assess competitive response or customer adoption. Patent position not publicly detailed.

**Patents:** Company holds patents in wet-process and plating technology. Specific portfolio size and strength not verifiable from public sources.

**Government / geopolitical moat:** SCHMID is a German company. Its back-shoring narrative (supporting European and US fabs relocating from Asia) aligns with CHIPS Act and EU Chips Act incentives, though SCHMID is not a direct beneficiary of US CHIPS Act funding (that targets fabs, not equipment makers). Indirect beneficiary of the back-shoring capex wave.

**Score: 1/1** — Defensible technology lead in PLP horizontal processing. SCHMID has a credible first-mover position in the specific format ranges it has delivered into the US and Asian markets. ## SECTION 10 — SECULAR AND CYCLICAL TAILWINDS

**Secular (10-year structural):**

The demand for advanced PCB and IC substrate manufacturing equipment is structurally driven by three irreversible trends:
1. AI compute scaling: more GPUs per rack, more complex board architectures, more HDI layers. This does not reverse at any foreseeable horizon.
2. Advanced packaging transition: the move from organic ABF substrates to glass-core packages is a 10-year technology migration that requires new equipment for each generation.
3. Back-shoring / friend-shoring: Europe and the US rebuilding domestic PCB and semiconductor packaging capacity. SCHMID is European-headquartered and culturally aligned with this trend.

The advanced packaging equipment market is projected to grow from approximately $4 billion in 2024 to over $12 billion by 2032 at a CAGR of approximately 14% (Yole Group / industry estimates).

Would demand survive a 30% AI capex cut? SCHMID's equipment goes into new capacity — capex cut would delay orders but not eliminate the long-term demand. Equipment makers feel cycles more acutely than the end market. A 30% AI capex cut in 2026 would likely collapse SCHMID's order intake by 40–60% for 2–3 quarters before recovery.

**Cyclical (1–3 year near-term):**

SCHMID just exited a severe trough driven by 2024 Chinese market weakness, solar demand collapse, and self-inflicted balance sheet uncertainty. The H2 2025 recovery is the start of a cyclical upturn driven by AI server board capex accelerating.

The upcycle is real. AI infrastructure capex from hyperscalers remains above $200 billion annually and is still growing. PCB fabricators are ordering new capacity now to meet 2027 delivery targets. SCHMID is in the early innings of this upcycle as an equipment provider.

Upcycle likely lasts through 2027–2028 before normalisation. AI capex itself is probably not cyclical in the traditional sense, but equipment orders will cluster as fabs place capacity bets.

**Score: 1/1** — Both secular (advanced packaging, AI compute scaling, back-shoring) and cyclical (AI server board capex upcycle, post-trough recovery) tailwinds are present and compounding. Quantitative support is adequate.

## SECTION 13 — ADVERSARIAL TESTING: STEEL-MAN THE BEAR CASE

**1. Thesis killer:** The single most credible failure scenario is a balance sheet collapse. The company has a going concern warning, €14 million in convertible notes maturing January 2028, ongoing share issuances to cover liabilities, and a CFO appointed January 2026 who has no established public track record. If Q2 2026 order intake disappoints materially vs. the €114 million annual target, the liquidity position deteriorates, the 2025 20-F is filed late again (which management has already signalled as likely), and the convertible note holders accelerate or convert aggressively, the company could face a dilution spiral that destroys equity value independent of the operational story. This is a 2024–2025 level revenue collapse — not impossible, but the AI server board order momentum makes it unlikely in the near term.

**2. Short report reconciliation:** No short report exists. The bear case is primarily structural (balance sheet, governance) rather than fraud-based. The Pomerantz investigation is unlikely to produce a material finding given the issues are operational (late filings) rather than accounting fraud.

**3. Substitute threat:** MKS Instruments (Atotech) is building directly competitive products at scale with a stronger balance sheet and global chemistry + equipment integration that SCHMID cannot match. Lam Research is entering panel-level processing from the semiconductor side with effectively unlimited capex. These are credible, funded competitors. The 12–18 month SCHMID PLP lead could evaporate within 24 months if Lam or MKS prioritise the segment.

**4. Concentration stress test:** If the largest unnamed Asian PCB customer (implied to represent a significant portion of H2 2025 AI-server-board orders) were lost — either to a competitor or through a Chinese regulatory action on that customer — revenue impact could be 20–35% of 2026 guidance. At €100 million revenue falling to €65–80 million and current EV of approximately €400 million, the EV/Revenue multiple blows out and the stock would likely test the 52-week low.

**5. Technology skip risk:** Glass-core substrates could displace organic ABF substrates faster than expected, rendering SCHMID's core HDI wet-process equipment less relevant for the most advanced packaging tiers. SCHMID is pursuing the glass-core equipment angle (TGV, QuantumLine), but if the transition happens in 2027 rather than 2029, SCHMID's panel-level packaging work could be disrupted by the next technology step.

**6. Balance sheet risk:** Cash position not publicly confirmed for Q1 2026 (2025 20-F not yet filed). The going concern warning implies thin cash relative to near-term obligations. €14 million convertible notes mature January 2028. Quarterly EBITDA at 13% margin on €18–25 million quarterly revenue is approximately €2.3–3.2 million — insufficient to service both operations and the convertible note without the operating cash flow from H2 2025's stronger quarters. Runway is tight. The risk of an additional dilutive raise in H2 2026 is real if Q2 order intake disappoints.

**7. Structural vs. temporary:** SCHMID's chokepoint position is real but 18–36 months in duration, not 5–10 years. The specific PLP format advantage will be competed away. The glass-core opportunity is genuinely long-duration but SCHMID is not the only player. The secular tailwind is structural; SCHMID's specific position within it is not permanent.

**8. Capex cut scenario:** A 40% AI hyperscaler capex cut would likely reduce SCHMID's order intake by 50–60% (equipment amplifies end-market cycles). At €60 million revenue and breakeven EBITDA, the company returns to 2024 levels with a worse balance sheet. This scenario would likely trigger another going concern warning.

**Overall bear case rating: STRONG**

The bear case is strong because it combines a credible operational scenario (capex cut or customer loss) with a genuinely fragile balance sheet. In most Serenity-framework companies, a strong technology thesis overcomes operational risk. Here, the going concern and serial governance failures mean the operational risk cannot be discounted. The bull case can still win — it requires the AI server order momentum to continue through Q2–Q3 2026 as management guides, enabling the company to generate sufficient cash to stabilise the balance sheet before the January 2028 convertible maturity. That is a conditional win, not a structural win.

## SECTION 14 — GEOPOLITICAL DIMENSION

**China exposure:**
- Manufacturing operations at Schmid Technology (Guangdong) Co., Ltd. in Zhongshan, China.

**Export control risk:** SCHMID's wet-process equipment for advanced PCB manufacturing is not itself directly subject to current US semiconductor export controls (those target wafer fab equipment and EDA software). However:
- If the US expands export controls to include advanced PCB manufacturing equipment (possible given the AI-server-board strategic sensitivity), SCHMID's ability to sell to Chinese customers could be restricted.
- Chinese customers of SCHMID may themselves be subject to US entity list designations, which would restrict SCHMID from servicing their equipment if SCHMID is shipping from the US or using US-origin components.

**Friendshoring beneficiary:** Yes. SCHMID is explicitly pursuing European and US backshoring orders. The March 2026 InfinityLine H+ delivery to "a leading U.S. technology company" is the first concrete evidence of this strategy bearing fruit. EU Chips Act and allied national semiconductor strategies create genuine demand for European equipment suppliers in domestic fab projects.

**Geopolitical verdict: MIXED / MODERATE HEADWIND**

China is both a meaningful revenue source and an escalating risk. The IP infringement lawsuit, potential export control expansion, and customer-side entity list risk are all live concerns. The back-shoring tailwind is real but will not fully offset Chinese customer loss if that exposure is restricted. Rating as moderate headwind rather than full headwind because the AI server board demand exists in both Chinese and non-Chinese geographies, and SCHMID is actively diversifying.
