## SECTION 0 — THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ TEST

1.  **Upstream Layer:** Advanced optronics, high-power diode lasers, military-grade radar arrays, kinetic barrels, and carbon-fibre structural mounts.
2.  **EOS's Position:** EOS integrates these components into high-precision Remote Weapon Systems (RWS) and directed energy systems. With the acquisition of MARSS, EOS integrates its effectors with the **NiDAR** command-and-control (C2) software platform.
3.  **Downstream Layer:** Prime defence contractors (Northrop Grumman, KNDS, ROKETSAN) and sovereign defence forces (Australia, Ukraine, Middle Eastern nations). These customers integrate EOS effectors and NiDAR command brains into naval vessels, armoured combat vehicles, and border security networks.
4.  **Hyperscaler/AI Infrastructure Link:** Protecting sovereign cloud datacenters, communication lines, and gigawatt-scale power stations from physical drone intrusion.
5.  **Disruption Impact:** If EOS disappeared tomorrow, NATO's primary supply of lightweight remote weapon stations for uncrewed vehicle integration halts. Replacing the NiDAR platform at over 60 critical infrastructure sites requires a complete redesign of the multi-sensor perimeter shield, a process taking 18–24 months.
6.  **Competitors:** Kongsberg (Norway), Leonardo (Italy), Rafael (Israel), and Aselsan (Turkey).
7.  **Strait of Hormuz Flow:** EOS holds a estimated 25% global market share in lightweight remote weapon stations. For AI-driven integrated C-UAS command-and-control platforms protecting national critical infrastructure in the Middle East, MARSS NiDAR holds a dominant position (>50% share).
8.  **Switching Costs:** Extremely high. Ripping out an integrated NiDAR perimeter security system requires re-qualifying alternative sensors and effectors, taking 12–18 months of security validation.

**Required Verdict: PARTIAL CHOKEPOINT** (Sovereign defence forces and critical infrastructure sites cannot easily replace NiDAR command-and-control).
