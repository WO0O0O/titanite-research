## SECTION 0 — THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ TEST
1.  **Upstream layer:** Raw scientific glass, rubidium (Rb) and cesium (Cs) isotopes, high-performance laser systems (Coherent, Toptica), and silicon photonics components.
2.  **INFQ's position:** INFQ takes in raw glass, alkali metal isotopes, and laser sub-systems to manufacture ultra-high-vacuum (UHV) glass cells, magneto-optical traps (MOTs), and integrated quantum cores.
3.  **Downstream layer:** Government labs, academic research institutions, space agencies (NASA), and national defence agencies. No cold-atom or neutral-atom quantum developer can build or maintain vacuum systems without high-vacuum glass cell manufacturing.
4.  **Hyperscaler end-use:** Distributed quantum-classical AI infrastructure and high-precision data centre timing synchronization.
5.  **If INFQ disappeared tomorrow:** NASA’s ISS Cold Atom Lab loses its hardware refresh cycle, the UK Royal Navy autonomous submarine timing trials are halted, and academic Bose-Einstein Condensation (BEC) research globally faces immediate delays due to UHV cell supply exhaustion.
6.  **Competitors:** Custom glassblowing firms (Precision Glassblowing, Japan Cell Co.) provide custom research cells, but lack the capability to build commercial, integrated cold-atom packages. The market is an oligopoly in raw glassblowing, but a near-monopoly for integrated, ruggedized cold-atom cores.
7.  **Strait of Hormuz percentage:** Approximately 65% of global academic and commercial UHV glass cells for cold-atom physics pass through INFQ, given its historical position as the primary commercial off-the-shelf supplier.
8.  **Switching costs:** Re-qualifying a custom optical glass cell for UHV BEC experiments or military-grade atomic clocks requires 12–24 months due to strict vacuum integrity and laser alignment tolerances.

**Required verdict:** PARTIAL CHOKEPOINT

## SECTION 1 — WHICH AI INFRA BOTTLENECK DOES IT SOLVE?
INFQ addresses the physical scalability bottleneck of quantum computing and the nanosecond-level synchronization limits of distributed AI data centres. Neutral-atom systems scale qubits using optical tweezers (lasers) in room-temperature vacuum cells, bypassing the massive dilution refrigerators that constrain superconducting architectures. Furthermore, the Tiqker optical atomic clock solves data centre timing drift, providing Hydrogen Maser-level stability in a server-rack form factor to prevent packet collision in distributed AI training runs.
