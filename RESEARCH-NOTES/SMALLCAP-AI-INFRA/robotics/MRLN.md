## SECTION 0 — THE STRAIT OF HORMUZ TEST

1.  **Upstream Layer:** Aircraft airframe OEMs (Lockheed Martin for the C-130J, Boeing for the KC-135, and Textron for the Cessna Caravan) and avionics hardware providers (Honeywell, GE Aerospace).
2.  **Merlin's Position:** Developer of the Merlin Pilot, an aircraft-agnostic autonomous flight control system. Merlin retrofits existing airframes with integrated hardware and software.
3.  **Downstream Layer:** Military operators (USSOCOM, U.S. Air Force) and commercial cargo carriers (Ameriflight, Dynamic Aviation). These operators cannot run single-pilot or zero-pilot operations without Merlin's software.
4.  **Hyperscaler End-Use:** Autonomous air cargo logistics networks, resolving pilot shortages and airlift capacity bottlenecks.
5.  **Supply Chain Disruption:** If Merlin disappeared tomorrow, USSOCOM C-130J and USAF KC-135 autonomy programmes would halt. Ameriflight's fleet conversion would freeze. Redesigning these systems for alternative software requires two to four years due to regulatory certification timelines.
6.  **Competitors:** Aurora Flight Sciences (Boeing), Reliable Robotics, Xwing (acquired by Joby Aviation), and Pyka.
7.  **Global Flow Percentage:** Merlin represents a partial chokepoint. It is one of only three companies in active FAA/CAA certification pathways for Part 135 flight autonomy, and the only player under a prime USSOCOM C-130J contract.
8.  **Switching Costs:** Three to five years. Replacing the software requires physical redesign of the avionics interface, re-writing flight control algorithms, and repeating the Civil Aviation Authority and FAA certification processes from scratch.

**Verdict: PARTIAL CHOKEPOINT.**
